How we observe the sun.

SOHO - Solar and Heliospheric Observatory

Launched in 1995, SOHO was one of the first spacecraft designed specifically to study the Sun continuously from space, revolutionizing solar physics. It provided the first consistent, real-time monitoring of coronal mass ejections (CMEs), becoming a critical early-warning system for space weather. SOHO has offered over two decades of uninterrupted solar data, covering nearly two full solar cycles. Stationed at the L1 Lagrange Point, SOHO provides a stable vantage point for observing both the Sun and its effects on Earth. Unexpectedly, SOHO has become the greatest comet discoverer in history, identifying over 4,000 comets through its LASCO coronagraph.

SOHO Orbiting the Sun-Earth Lagrange Point 1 (L1) where it has a continuous uninterrupted view of the Sun.

Parker Solar Probe

The Parker Solar Probe, launched by NASA on August 12, 2018, is the first spacecraft to fly directly into the Sun’s corona—the outermost part of its atmosphere. Named after astrophysicist Dr. Eugene Parker, who theorized the existence of the solar wind in 1958, the mission aims to unravel longstanding mysteries about the Sun's behavior. To accomplish this, Parker uses a series of seven Venus gravity assists to gradually spiral closer to the Sun, ultimately reaching speeds over 690,000 km/h (430,000 mph)—making it the fastest human-made object in history.

Parker Solar Probe Orbiting the Sun independently from earth using Venus Gravity Assist

STEREO - Solar Terrestrial Relations Observatory

The STEREO mission, launched by NASA in 2006, consists of two nearly identical spacecraft—STEREO-Ahead and STEREO-Behind—that orbit the Sun ahead of and behind Earth. Their unique positions provide stereoscopic 3D views of the Sun, allowing scientists to observe solar phenomena like coronal mass ejections (CMEs) from multiple angles. This unprecedented perspective has greatly improved our understanding of how CMEs propagate through space and interact with Earth’s magnetic environment, enhancing space weather forecasting capabilities. Sadly, in 2014 NASA lost contact with STEREO B, and has been unable to regain connection.

In February 2011 STEREO A and B reached a 90 degree separation.
