Coronal Mass Ejections (CMEs)

What is a Coronal Mass Ejection? (CME)

A Coronal Mass Ejection (CME) is a massive burst of solar wind and magnetic fields rising above the solar corona or being released into space. These powerful eruptions originate from the Sun's outer atmosphere, often following solar flares or other signs of heightened solar activity. CMEs occur when magnetic field lines near sunspots become twisted and unstable, suddenly releasing enormous amounts of plasma and energy. This ejected material travels through the solar system and can interact with Earth's magnetosphere, potentially disrupting satellite communications, GPS systems, and even power grids. The frequency and intensity of CMEs are closely tied to the 11-year solar cycle, a natural period of increasing and decreasing solar activity. During solar maximum—the peak of this cycle—the Sun exhibits more sunspots and a higher likelihood of CMEs. In contrast, solar minimum is a quieter phase with fewer eruptions. Understanding CMEs and the solar cycle is essential for space weather forecasting and protecting technology on Earth and in orbit.
